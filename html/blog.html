Past Lives
July 17, 2023

“If you had never left Seoul, would I have still looked for you? Would we have dated? Broken up? Gotten married? Would we have had kids together?”

I watched Past Lives with my friends at our local theater on a Tuesday night. It's about three people: a Korean immigrant woman, her childhood sweetheart, and her husband. I first heard about this movie when I stumbled across it's trailer on YouTube. It was an A24 film. Indie. So I knew it was going to be good. The first thing that captivated me was the somber and nostalgic atmosphere of the story. Where the film gently guides you in the slow burn journey of the connection between two people, who both ponder about their “what if?” together. What if they had stayed together in Seoul, Korea? What if she never left to America? What if they had stayed in contact when they first reunited through Skype? Because a “them” never happened, there's now a million branches of what-ifs.



The next thing that stood out to me was when Na Young, our story's main woman, explained to her husband about 인연 • (inyeon), which describes the ties between two people over thousands of past lives. This particular word was what had me listening to the trailer on loop for a day. I'm not even exaggerating. I think it's because Buddhism has a similar sentiment about the connections between two people. And in Vietnamese, we have định mệnh, which directly translates to fate. But the twist is that we believe that whoever we get married to in our current life is because we owed them in our past life.

Our strong, faithful connections to each other is tied by debt we must pay back for previous interactions that overlapped beyond our lifespans.





Now, I'm not going to go too much into the plot because you can only feel the raw emotions poured out between the main characters when you watch the emotional, sentimental film. But I do want to briefly delve into how I resonated with Past Lives.

Going back to the idea of định mệnh, I grew up with the belief that there is a reason, one way or another, that you meet each person in your life. To me, this idea was kind of cool in the sense that I knew that I would be learning something important from each special and unique encounter. And it became even romantic when I applied it to talking to someone I liked.

A big part of me worries a lot about whether I can grow closer to this person or not. And how long this friendship could actually last without draining me in anxiety. But when I think about định mệnh again, I realize that I don't really have complete control over how long I can stay with someone. As I'm a person who believes in fate, I don't personally think that we can dictate our reality in such a way where we have the power to sway what has already been destined. And that means I shouldn't worry about fickle matters of how long I can keep in touch with a person if I couldn't bend the universe to my will in the first place.

And yet, I continue to entertain myself about the "what ifs" in my own life. What if I still stayed in my previous relationship? Would it have continued to stay stagnant? What if I replied when they reached out to me? Would we have become friends again? What if I tried pursuing a new relationship now? Would it bite me in the back? Would it end up severing our connection rather than strengthening it?

But right now, it's important to remember that I'm happy and content as things are now. And that those "what ifs" should stay intangible. I remember on the drive home from the movies, my friend admitted she related to the film. When she asked a friend about how to stop thinking about the alternative choice she could've pursued, they said "Don't think about them as what-ifs. Think about them as things that were not supposed to happen." A bit of a pragmatic approach, but it's definitely straight-forward.

So if things were meant to be, then I think life will forge those paths for us. And if they were not meant to be, then you have to let go. Just like what the ending of Past Lives told the audience.

Social Interactions
June 23, 2023

Have you ever heard of the MBTI personality test? It's a series of questions that are supposed to reveal your "core personality type". Of course, this is just as much pseudoscience as analyzing people's astrology signs. But it's fun to see how "compatible" you are with your friends!

Anyways, the reason why I am bringing it up is because my MBTI is INFP, which stands for Introverted, Intuitive, Feeling, and Perspective. Basically someone who is very quiet and reserved. While I'd like to say I have a lot of friends, I only keep a handful close to me. And even then, I have a time limit on how long I can hang out with people. By the 3 hour mark, I pretty much keep my mouth shut because I generally don't have much to say and I rather listen to people talk. Talking genuinely tires me out because I tend to overthink what I want to say and end up exerting too much effort to carry a simple conversation sometimes.

That's why when people turn their full attention on me unexpectedly, it freaks me out. I get really anxious and feel like an alarm is about to go off if the attention doesn't move somewhere else. I just feel like I never really know what to do. Thankfully, I don't deal with it often. I'd say I'm pretty good at blending in the background and timing my input correctly when others are talking.

BUT I wouldn't say that being introverted is bad. I used to think that way, but I realized people are glad and appreciative of us introverts when we sit back and listen to them talk. People like us need to exist in this loud, loud world! Yet even so, I find myself in unfortunate dilemmas where I'm calling a friend, and the conversation falls silent, and I panic because I don't know what else to say. I want the conversation to keep flowing, but it's hard for me to find a common point of interest to pick up the talk again. At moments like this, I regret never learning how to be a better conversationist.

Interestingly, whenever I express my inner conflict to people, they usually say that silence is fine and sometimes necessary. But I can't help but worry regardless. When I observe my friends, they're all great at carry a fluid conversation (with witty humor too) and are just, so easy to talk to. I honestly wish I could give back the same energy to them so that we can all have a super fun time together. But alas, I was not gifted with being a smooth talker. I should try to be more content with who I am instead.

Taking Criticism
May 27, 2023

Hello, hello! I'm currently vibing to the first Spider-Verse movie playing in the background as I wait for the sequel to come out in the theaters (coming soon on June 1st!!!).
Anyways, as you can guess from the title, this week's blog is going to be a slight turn from the normal optimistic approach. So let's head right into it.

Now, what you're going to read is very typical of anyone, especially Asian, who has/had strict parents. Growing up, I feared criticism in my work the most. From turning in an essay that took me a week to finish, or showing off an artwork to my artsy friends, to sharing a piece of code that I wrote, already aware of how inefficient or messy compared to others. Despite being raised with the expectation of earning 100's when I was little, my work had always been far from perfect. Sure, I had straight A's on my report card, but my work always needed something to improve. My essays, for example, were given back to me in C's or B's. My friends, on the other hand, were gifted with a natural flow in their writing and often earned A's from just spending an all-nighter on the paper. And I remember shying away from looking at what I got wrong and what areas needed to be fixed...

The critiques embarrassed me and made me ashamed of myself. Even as I grew up, I never got to feel comfortable with my errors, much less hearing a teacher or a friend verbally tell me what I need to change. It just makes me feel like a little kid again, like I need to be sat down while they carefully jump around words to avoid hurting my feelings, though I try to be appreciative of the feedback. Going back on the expectation part, I do remember my mom sighing in disappointment every time she saw my papers and assignments marked up in red pen. I faintly remember her telling me I was a not so bright child, or maybe that was my inner thoughts telling me so? I think those kind of thoughts have stuck to me like glue all the way to now.

Either way, even as my parents became a lot less lenient when I entered high school and college, the lingering feelings of shame continued to follow me. I'd like to say I am a lot better at handling it now (I used to tear up at the tiniest commments). But even as my peers and I tell myself that mistakes are fine and perfectly okay and that everyone makes mistakes, it's still hard for me to stomach my errors. Which, in turn, leads me to keep making the same mistake because I end up avoiding how to learn from it.

That's not to say I see criticism in a pessimistic view. I really do try to accept it as much as I can because I know it's important to my growth as an individual. If I want to do better, then I need the tools to do better, and sometimes that comes from listening to what others have to say about my work. Will I ever overcome this fear? I think so. But it will take time as I build the courage to embrace and reflect on my mistakes to learn from them.
